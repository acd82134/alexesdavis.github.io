About Me

Five years ago, as a high school senior, I found myself inundated in fear and anxiety as I began to explore my options as an upcoming first-generation college student. Despite my engulfment in apprehension regarding post-secondary education, my interest in attending the University of Georgia shined through the abyss of uncertainty and self-doubt. I was enchanted by the perpetuity of greatness that the university exemplifies through the pillars of wisdom, justice, and moderation. Additionally, a Saturday spent between the hedges certainly piqued my interest and further fueled my excitement!

Presently, as a senior psychology student, I can admit that while my major and career interests have changed over the years, my sentiments of admiration and respect for this university have remained unscathed. With a Mental Health Counseling program that is well-rounded through its emphasis on social justice and highly reputable with a top three nationwide ranking, the decision to stay at this illustrious university is both simple and self-evident.Amid the exemplary graduate programs available at the University of Georgia, I personally resonate the most with the Mental Health Counseling program because I am passionate about helping marginalized populations rediscover their joy and power. I have dreams of advocating for change in available resources and mental health policies that could significantly reduce the staggering disparities seen today amongst varying socioeconomic and racial groups. It is a harrowing reality that mental health incongruencies exist within intersectional groups, such as individuals with disabilities, the homeless, women, and nearly every racial minority. My career interests of lowering the percentage of untreated mental illnesses amongst incarcerated African American's in juvenile detention centers, aligns with the sustainable service demonstrated by the Mental Health Counseling programs focus on social equitability.

As Oprah Winfrey once said, Where there is no struggle, there is no strength. I love this quote because I know first-hand what it means to struggle. From a retroactive hardship withdrawal to working three jobs as a full-time student, my college career has irrefutably contained some of my largest tribulations to date. A further consequence of being pulled in so many different directions by the stressors of life was the decline of my GPA. Even in the face of personal and familial hardships, I welcomed the challenge and used my desire for success as fuel to change the course of my academic path. With a newly invigorated tenacity for school, I was able to earn a spot on the Dean's List for the Summer of 2018, work as an intern for both Focus+Fragile Kids and Gwinnett United in Drug Education, chosen to serve as one of the 36 representatives of the Arch Society and inducted as a member of Alpha Kappa Alpha Sorority Incorporated.

My internship responsibilities included supervising a group of 15 children with developmental and/or physical disabilities, presenting feedback on Gwinnett county's compliance check rates for the quarter, and recording a radio infomercial regarding the prevalence of prescription drug abuse in the youth today. Through my membership with the Arch society, serving as an Athens Clarke Middle School mentor and volunteering as a certified peer support specialist for iPrevail Mental Health Solutions, I am honored to have been granted the responsibility to humbly serve my community. Direct examples of my service in these organizations include acting as an official representative and goodwill ambassador of the University of Georgia, facilitating the beginning of the end of isolated children in our school systems, and contributing to easily accessible mental health treatment for individuals worldwide. As an up and coming helping professional, what better way to assist others achieve wellness and equality than through humble service. As an Alpha Kappa Alpha sorority member, it is my goal to embody our motto, By culture, by merit, through selfless action to anyone in need. I know in my heart that I have what it takes to thrive in this graduate program through my resilience in my undergraduate career as well as my overwhelming desire to be of service to all mankind.
